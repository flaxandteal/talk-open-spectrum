  • The Cornish: Pear's Soap, high-pressure steam engine, the one true fake pasty.
  • Mine adventurers, large scale deep mining. Black & white flags. Cusp of first industrial revolution. However, mines go below the water table, and a major limitation was the ability to get water _out_ of mines. This was the first true task of the steam era.
  • In 1765, James Watt was working at a University and spotted inefficiencies in the steam engine; he a developed a PoC, spent most of his cash on patenting, and, in 1775, his original backer went bankrupt and an angel investor, Boulton, stepped in to help get him a crack dev team, facilities and build an MVP. A year later, he'd some machines in production, and by 1777 his machines were spreading through the Cornish mining industry, the first true test of steam at scale. Much of installation was at cost, and adventurers paid Boulton & Watt based on their closed IP managed service agreement.
  • However, Boulton & Watt's IP claim was broad. It covered all new steam engines, creating a block on independent improvements. They charged steep subscriptions, and when mines were least productive, in the rainy winter, adventurers were paying the highest infrastructure costs, with no clear caps. After the labour in getting the patent, Watt & Boulton did not sublicense and blocked all potential competitors, and all other innovation for almost a quarter of a century.
  • The adventurers debated a legal challenge, but finally decided it was unenforceable and started secretly building "pirate engines", thanks to one of the key co-innovators that had made Watt's business possible, James Wilkinson. When discovered, both sides sunk huge time and money, with royalties being stuck in escrow, into trying to get past the IP issue.
  • In 1800, the patent ran out. For 10 years, the adventurers just soaked up the benefits of the vanished expense, but then they started getting creative, and practised what they had preached. It turns out Docker isn't the first tech to co-opt nautical terms for open IP projects. Their mines, presumably including the old pirate ones, had "captains", managers, who started an industry publication for all the captains and engineers to release technical improvements and improve best-practice standards industry-wide, as well as to generate a climate of competition between the engineers for publicly contributing innovation, by printing live performance stats for all the engines. An "engine reporter", Lean, was responsible for coordinating all this in that 19th Century Github. Meanwhile Trevithick, of Cornwall's Ding Dong Mine, who previously had an injunction from Watt hammered into his door, invented the high-pressure steam engine, crossing the power-to-weight barrier, and quickly enabling steam trains - he took no patent and instead sold his expertise internationally. Unfortunately for him, while in South America, he got pressed into Simon Bolivar's revolutionary army, but couldn't shoot straight and was eventually allowed to go home.
  • The mines created an IP-sharing culture, incorporating each others' innovations, stimulating professional self-improvement in engineers, and focused on their value-add as independent competing service and commodity providers, rather than trying to become technology monoliths. Cornwall's mines suddenly trebled their annual effiency gains, and kept that consistent annual rate of improvement for over thirty years.
  • Cornwall's steam innovations went on, thanks to Trevithick, as much as Watt, to become the engine of industrial revolution, and to revolutionize the globe.
